What to Build Before Your First Ten Customers

Most early-stage products fail at scoping in one of two directions: either they ship something so thin it can't hold a real customer's trust, or they spend months building features that only matter once there are hundreds of users. The right scope for the first ten customers is narrower — and more specific — than either instinct suggests.

Build for the person, not the market

At ten customers, there's no "average user" yet — there are ten specific people with specific problems. The highest-leverage move is talking to them directly and building only what unblocks their actual next step, rather than guessing at a broader market's needs. Features that make sense at scale — admin dashboards, granular permissions, elaborate settings — can almost always wait.

The three things that can't wait

Regardless of the product, three things tend to matter from day one: a core flow that works reliably every time, a way to know when something breaks before the customer tells you, and a simple way to collect feedback without it getting lost. Skipping any of these to move faster usually costs more time later, chasing down issues blind.

What's safe to fake

Plenty of "backend" work can be done manually behind the scenes at this stage — onboarding a customer by hand, generating a report manually instead of building a reporting engine, answering support requests directly instead of building a help center. This isn't cutting corners; it's spending engineering time only where automation actually saves more than it costs right now.

Knowing when to stop being manual

The signal to formalize a manual process isn't a specific customer count — it's when doing it by hand starts taking time away from talking to customers or building the next real improvement. That's usually a clearer, more honest trigger than an arbitrary roadmap date.
